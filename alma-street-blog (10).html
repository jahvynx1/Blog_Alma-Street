Chapter One

The Mill That Built a City

The true origin of Vancouver does not begin with a blueprint for a metropolis, but with the scent of cedar and the mechanical whine of a blade. In 1865, the Hastings Mill was established on the south shore of Burrard Inlet, long before the name "Vancouver" held any legal weight. What began as a primitive industrial outpost quickly evolved into a self-sustaining ecosystem. The mill was more than an employer; it was a lifeline. At its heart sat the General Store, a singular structure that functioned as the settlement's post office, its first library, and its only social sanctuary. In an era of profound isolation, this store was the main agency for both mail and the essential human connection that transformed a transient labor camp into a rooted community.

By 1886, the burgeoning settlement was poised for greatness, only to be met by the Great Vancouver Fire. In a matter of minutes, the newly incorporated city was reduced to ash, yet the mill and its resilient general store defied the flames. This survival was not merely a stroke of luck — it served as a physical and symbolic anchor for a displaced population. The investigation into Alma Street's DNA reveals that the "seed" of the city was planted here, in the interplay between industrial necessity and the social infrastructure of the mill settlement. It proves that Vancouver was not built by urban planners, but by a sawmill that refused to burn, providing the foundational stability from which a modern timber empire, and eventually a city, could grow.

Chapter Two

From Mill to Museum

After surviving the Great Vancouver Fire of 1886, Hastings Mill continued to grow and became one of the most important industrial operations in the city. For decades it supported jobs, trade, and the expansion of Vancouver as a major port. However, by the late 1920s the aging mill was no longer considered useful, and operations officially closed in 1928. City officials planned to demolish the structure as Vancouver focused on modernization and urban growth.

A group called the Native Daughters of British Columbia opposed this decision and fought to preserve the building because of its historical significance. In 1930, they organized an extraordinary rescue effort by dismantling the mill, placing it on a barge, and towing it across English Bay to Alma Street. This dramatic relocation transformed the mill from an industrial workplace into a symbol of Vancouver's origins.

Once rebuilt, the structure became a museum that allowed residents to connect with the city's early history. Its survival shows how community action can shape what a city chooses to remember. The mill's journey from economic powerhouse to preserved landmark reflects Vancouver's broader transition from frontier settlement to modern urban centre.

Chapter Three

The Street Comes Alive

The neighbourhood attracted a diverse mix: university faculty and students from UBC (founded in 1925, just a short distance away), craftspeople, merchants, artists, and working families who valued being close to the ocean and the park. There was an intellectualism here — a love of ideas — that mingled naturally with the physical beauty of the setting: cliffs above English Bay, the mountains in the distance, the old mill building keeping quiet watch.

The Places That Defined the Street

🎷

Alma St. Café

More than just a café — the Alma St. Café was one of Vancouver's beloved jazz venues. Listed by the Coastal Jazz and Blues Society as a key part of the city's jazz history, it was a place where the music and the coffee were equally memorable. Jazz was the language of cool here: improvisational, alive, and utterly unforgettable on a warm evening.

🥐

Bon Ton Pastry & Confectionery

The most divine French pastries in the neighbourhood. Elegant food with an artisan's touch — the kind of place where the smell of fresh bread drew you in from half a block away, and the croissants made you feel briefly like you were somewhere in Paris.

Alma Street in the 1950s — a neighbourhood alive with character

The people were defined by their relationship to the street itself. Neighbours knew each other. You'd see the same faces at the bakery, hear the same musicians at the jazz club. In an era before screens dominated daily life, the street itself was the platform — for connection, for commerce, for culture.

The proximity to the oldest building in Vancouver gave residents something rare: a daily reminder of where it all began. The old mill store at the foot of Alma Street was not just a museum — it was a living piece of the city's DNA, a cedar-plank anchor to a time when this entire landscape was forest and shoreline and possibility.

During the 1940s and into the post-war years, Kitsilano and the streets around Alma saw a significant shift. Many of the older family homes along the slope above English Bay were converted into rooming houses during World War II, and for the next two decades they remained that way — affordable, lively, and welcoming to a younger generation. By the late 1950s and into the 1960s, university students and artists had claimed the neighbourhood as their own.

Alma Street sat at the intersection of all of this energy. The professors from UBC on one side, the bohemian artists on the other, the working families in the middle — and underneath it all, the quiet, weathered presence of the oldest building in the city, sitting at the foot of the street like an elder who had seen everything and forgotten nothing. Residents who grew up near Alma in those decades describe a childhood spent within walking distance of the ocean, the park, the bakery, and the jazz club. That kind of completeness — a whole life contained within a few blocks — is something that modern city planning still aspires to but rarely achieves.

The Alma St. Café was a cornerstone of that musical life — a spot where jazz wasn't just background noise but the main event. Recognized by the Coastal Jazz and Blues Society as part of Vancouver's jazz heritage, it drew musicians and listeners together in a way that felt genuinely communal. Just down the street, Bon Ton Pastry & Confectionery — founded all the way back in 1926 — was already a Vancouver institution. Originally on Granville Street, the beloved bakery eventually made its way to the Kitsilano area, where its famous Diplomat Cake, layered with flaky puff pastry and rum-soaked sponge, became the stuff of legend. Both places gave Alma Street its texture: one fed the soul, the other fed the stomach.

Chapter Four

The People and the Community

Alma Street was shaped by postwar immigration, affordable housing, work opportunities, and proximity to UBC. From the 1950s to the 1970s, it attracted Greek immigrants, South Asian families, and UBC students alongside counterculture youth. This diversity — working-class families, immigrants, and bohemian students — fostered a dynamic mixed community. Churches, temples, cafés, and community centres were the heart of neighbourhood life, while shared activism constructed a common identity. Over time, residents came to understand Alma Street as their home because it perfectly mixed affordability, social connection, and inclusivity, making it an accessible haven for people from all walks of life.

Migration and Community Growth

The postwar period brought highly diverse migration to Kitsilano. Greek immigrants settled along West Broadway and formed strong cultural and business ties that facilitated a thriving community. Punjabi immigrants were equally integral — many took up work in the sawmills, helped build Vancouver's early Gurdwaras, and created open spiritual and social connections throughout the neighbourhood. Meanwhile, the low rents in Kitsilano were deeply attractive to nearby UBC students and counterculture youth, helping the area grow into a hub for alternative lifestyles. Together, the interplay of youth counterculture and bustling immigrant communities built a historic neighbourhood where openness and diversity became pillars of its success.

Housing and Affordability

Central to the growth of Kitsilano and Alma Street was affordable housing. Older homes, small apartments, and rooming houses allowed immigrants, families, and students to live in the area. The dynamic of shared housing inherently promoted social interaction. While redevelopment occurred in the 1970s, the neighbourhood remained relatively accessible during this formative period.

Work and Economy

Early residents engaged in sawmill work and manual labour — this was particularly true of South Asian workers. Over time, employment shifted toward small business, services, and UBC-related jobs. This evolving mix of work supported and sustained a wide range of residents across generations.

Community and Cultural Life

Local institutions were cornerstones of the community. Greek Orthodox churches, Sikh temples, and neighbourhood centres offered cultural, social, and supportive services. Beyond these formal institutions, informal spaces like cafés, parks, and beaches held equal significance. Kitsilano Beach and the local shops surrounding it became everyday meeting points where different groups mixed freely and openly.

"In spite of cultural differences, many residents could relate on their economic backgrounds, which shaped connections as intimate as their cultural roots." — Community history of Kitsilano and the Alma Street corridor

Alma Street was able to become home to so many because of its capacity to mix affordability, diversity, and community. Immigrants, workers, and students all left a lasting mark — building a neighbourhood that was inclusive and shared, and as such, a place that truly belonged to everyone.

Chapter Five

Then and Now: Jo's Italian Deli

Jo's Italian Deli arrived on Alma Street in 2021, opening its first-ever Vancouver location at 2505 Alma Street. The deli had started modestly in Chilliwack in 2018 — a small family operation serving classic Italian sandwiches and fresh groceries — and quickly built a devoted following. The Vancouver outpost was an immediate hit. Today it holds a near-perfect rating on delivery platforms with over 2,000 reviews, and customers struggle to find words for how good the food is.

What makes Jo's feel at home on Alma Street is its philosophy. This is not a chain restaurant with a corporate identity — it is, in the truest sense, a family kitchen opened to the public. As the owners describe it: the menu centres on classic Italian sandwiches on ciabatta — including their beloved Italian cold cuts and spicy salami — and hearty fresh-made pastas like spaghetti and meatballs, rigatoni in slow-cooked meat sauce, and potato gnocchi in homemade pesto cream sauce. Every order comes with fresh focaccia. Nothing here is pretentious. Everything is made with care.

What People Are Saying

"Best Italian sandwich in Vancouver." · "Great Bolognese! Great portion size." · "Full and honest Italian food — warm, well-made tomato sauce." · "Magnificent!!" — A sampling of reviews from Jo's loyal customers, who describe the deli with the kind of enthusiasm usually reserved for places people have been going to their whole lives.

The deli's success has been remarkable. Since opening on Alma, Jo's has expanded with a second Vancouver concept — Mangia's Sandwiches on South Granville — and has a new Main Street location on the way. But it's the Alma Street location that started it all in Vancouver. And there is something deeply fitting about that. A family-owned Italian deli, serving honest food made from real recipes, on the oldest street in the city — it is, in many ways, exactly the kind of place Alma Street has always made room for.

A Street That Remembers

From a sawmill carved out of coastal forest in 1865, to a jazz club humming on a summer evening, to a family deli serving fresh focaccia today — Alma Street is not just a place on a map. It is a living record of how a city grows, who builds it, and what it chooses to keep. The oldest building in Vancouver still stands at the foot of this street, watching. Some things refuse to burn.
